Thurgood Marshall Bio

Thurgood Marshall was born in Baltimore, Maryland on July 2, 1908, Thurgood Marshall grandaddy was a slave. Thurgood Marshall attended Frederick Douglass high school. Marshall finished high school in 1925 and attended college at Lincoln University in pennsylvania, In 1930, while he was in college he enjoyed being on the debate team and he joined the Alpha phi Alpha fraternity. His classmates at Lincoln included a special group of future Black leaders such as the poet and author Langston Hughes, the future President of Ghana, Kwame Nkrumah, and musician Cab Calloway. Just before graduation, he married his first wife, Vivian "Buster" Burey. Their twenty-five year marriage ended with her death from cancer in 1955.

When Marshall graduating from Lincoln Marshall wanted to attend the university of Maryland but he was turned down because he was black. Instead Marshall went to law school at Howard University where he finished first in his class When marshall graduated law School, Marshall opened a small law practice in Baltimore. One of his first big cases was against the University of Maryland. Marshall remembered how they would not admit him because he was black. In 1935, he heard of another student, Donald Murray, who didn't get accepted because he was black. Marshall took the University of Maryland to court and won the case. Now they would have to let African-Americans attend the school. This was just the start of Marshall's fight against segregation.

Marshall Started to be notice for his skills as a lawyer and his desire for civil rights. He became the main lawyer for the NACCP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People). Then for the next several years marshall would travel around the globe and help African-american who was wrongly accused by defending them. He also fought against segregation and the Jim Crow laws of the South. He eventually earned the nickname "Mr. Civil Rights".

Marshall most famous case was Brown vs The board of education. In this case marshall argued that schools should not be segregated. In some states it was illegal for black kids to go to a white school. Marshall argued that separate school could not be equal. In a landmark decision for the civil right movement, marshall won the case showing that segregation in school was unconstitutional.

In 1961, Marshall was appointed as a judge on the United States Court of Appeals by President John F. Kennedy. He served there until 1965 when he became the United States Solicitor General. As Solicitor General he represented the federal government before the supreme court.

President Lyndon Johnson nominated Thurgood Marshall for the Supreme Court in 1966. He was confirmed by the Senate on August 30, 1967 and became the first African-American Supreme Court Justice. While serving on the Supreme Court, Marshall championed the rights of the individual. He served on the court for 24 years. He retired in 1991 and was replaced by another African-American judge, Clarence Thomas.

Thurgood Marshall died of heart failure on January 24, 1993. He left a mark of using the law and the Constitution to fight for the rights of all people. He broke down racial barriers, including achieving one of the highest positions in the government as a member of the Supreme Court.

  • His birth first name was Thoroughgood, but as a child Marshall got tired of having to write out such a long name. He shortened his name to Thurgood in the second grade.
  • While working as a lawyer he argued 32 cases before the Supreme Court and won 29 of them.
